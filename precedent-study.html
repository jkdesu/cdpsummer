Three Layers of Analysis

1. Ontological Analysis

A. Technical Components
  • Core Library (JavaScript-Based):
    The heart of p5.js is a JavaScript codebase that allows users to easily generate visuals, animation, and interaction directly in the browser. Its API mirrors the structure of Processing but is adapted for the web, using HTML5 canvas. It abstracts complex DOM and WebGL operations, making creative coding more accessible to beginners.
  • Editor and Runtime Environment:
    p5.js supports a "sketch" metaphor where each project is self-contained. The p5.js Web Editor allows users to write, run, and share sketches instantly online. This low-barrier environment removes the need for installing software or setting up complex development tools.
  • Modular System:
    The library is organized into modules (e.g., sound, typography, color, math, events), which users can combine depending on their needs. This modularity encourages experimentation and extensibility.
  • Integration with Web Ecosystem:
    Because it's browser-based, p5.js works natively with HTML, CSS, and other JS libraries (e.g., ml5.js for machine learning), encouraging hybrid creative practices that combine visual design and interaction.
  • Extension Libraries (Modular Add-ons):
    p5.js includes a flexible system for libraries that extend its core capabilities. These libraries are often developed by the community and allow for custom functionality, such as sound synthesis (p5.sound), brush-based drawing (p5.brush), ASCII conversion (p5.asciify), and UI tools (p5.5). This modular architecture supports experimentation and encourages users to build tools tailored to their specific creative or educational needs.
B. Conceptual Framework
  • Sketchbook Metaphor:
    Inspired by Processing, p5.js uses the idea of a "sketch" as a quick, expressive, and iterative creative unit. Each sketch starts with simple setup() and draw() functions, framing code as a playful and forgiving act rather than an engineering task.
  • Software as Culture:
    p5.js embeds values into its structure‚Äîcode is not neutral. Everything from naming conventions to documentation style reflects a political stance: one that is anti-exclusion, anti-hierarchy, and anti-speed-for-speed's-sake.
  • Care and Softness:
    Echoing Lauren Lee McCarthy's philosophy, the platform emphasizes emotional, ethical, and relational dimensions of coding. Concepts like soft coding, consent, and care are actively embedded into the way people engage with the tool and each other.
C. Social and Cultural Infrastructure
  • Processing Foundation:
    p5.js is supported by the Processing Foundation, linking it to a longer lineage of creative toolmaking. This foundation offers guidance, funding, and community stewardship, ensuring the sustainability of p5.js as a free resource.
  • Community Governance:
    Development is collaborative and transparent. Anyone can propose changes or contribute, and the core team‚Äîdiverse in geography, gender, and background‚Äîactively engages with contributors through GitHub, forums, and working groups.
  • Education and Documentation:
    The p5.js website offers extensive multilingual documentation, beginner tutorials, and educator guides. The tone is intentionally non-intimidating and welcoming, reinforcing the idea that everyone belongs in creative coding spaces.
  • Inclusive Defaults:
    From its built-in screen reader support to its commitment to internationalization, the infrastructure of p5.js centers people often left out of tech: non-English speakers, students without access to expensive tools, and those new to programming.
  • Non-commercial Ethos:
    There is no corporate monetization model behind p5.js. It is maintained by volunteers and grants, positioning it as a form of digital commons and resisting the commercialization of creativity.

Featured Project: LAUREN

One of Lauren Lee McCarthy's most notable projects, LAUREN, brings critical attention to contemporary issues of data usage and surveillance. In this work, McCarthy positions herself as a "human Alexa," installing a network of custom smart devices‚Äîcameras, microphones, locks, faucets, and appliances‚Äîin participants' homes and monitoring them 24/7. As described in the project statement: "LAUREN is a human Alexa, watching over you in your home and controlling it for you... Anything Alexa can do LAUREN can do better. LAUREN can understand you as a human and anticipate your needs and desires." By embodying the very systems she critiques, McCarthy exposes the uncomfortable tensions between intimacy and privacy, convenience and agency, and highlights the often-invisible human labor behind so-called "automated" technologies.

LAUREN is not just a commentary on smart home devices‚Äîit is a deeply personal performance that confronts users with the emotional and ethical dimensions of living under constant digital surveillance. It exemplifies McCarthy's larger practice of using design and performance art to ask difficult questions about who builds our technologies, whose needs they serve, and what kinds of futures they enable‚Äîor foreclose.

p5.js

p5.js was created in response to long-standing inequities in both the tech and art worlds. Its founder, Lauren Lee McCarthy, brings a unique perspective shaped by her technical background and critical reflection. Unlike traditional artists, McCarthy studied computer science at MIT, where she initially saw data as objective and indisputable. In a 2023 interview during her visit to Stanford's Institute for Human-Centered Artificial Intelligence, she recalled, "Early in my career, I thought of data as something more factual, or more unchanging, or indisputable... I have come to understand that data is something that is merely constructed in some way; you can't understand data as separate from the frame in which it was collected or analyzed or understood." This critical awareness of data as socially constructed became foundational to her work.

By 2024, McCarthy had begun to question not just data itself, but the deeper values embedded within the tools we use to process and represent it. She stated, "We have to question what values are being built into the software we use. A piece of technology is not neutral; it's built based on the biases, assumptions and priorities of the people that create it." She pointed out that many of these tools emerge from environments like Silicon Valley, shaped by predominantly white, male, and Western perspectives. This critique directly informed the development of p5.js, which prioritizes accessibility, inclusivity, and community‚Äîchallenging traditional software paradigms. "We also need to question: how is this being made? And how much agency do we have to decide whether and how we want to use it?" In her performances and teaching, McCarthy often explores themes of agency, control, and surveillance, further blurring the line between artist, user, and programmer.

  • Lack of Representation: Lauren Lee McCarthy saw that women, people of color, and people with disabilities were consistently underrepresented in creative coding spaces. Unlike tools that treat diversity as an afterthought, p5.js was built from the ground up with equity in mind.
  • Dominance of Corporate Tech Culture: Most software is shaped by western, male, Silicon Valley engineers. McCarthy challenged the idea that tools should be optimized only for speed or efficiency‚Äîinstead prioritizing care, softness, and accessibility.
  • Response to Processing: While p5.js grew from the Processing project (also meant for visual code-based experimentation), p5.js made a decisive leap into web-based, inclusive, and explicitly political toolmaking.
  • Educational Context: The library was released in a moment where STEM-to-STEAM (adding art to science/tech/math) education was gaining momentum. p5.js became an important bridge, especially in K‚Äì12 and university classrooms around the world.
  • Community-first Philosophy: In contrast to capitalist narratives of "innovation," p5.js centers slow growth, translation efforts, consent-based participation, and open collaboration.

p5.js was a tool reshaping what creative freedom and inclusivity could look like in code.

Foundation of Processing to p5.js

The roots of p5.js trace back to the early 2000s with the creation of Processing, a Java-based visual programming language founded in 2001 by Casey Reas and Ben Fry. Developed at the MIT Media Lab under the guidance of John Maeda, Processing was intended to make coding accessible to artists, designers, and creatives by simplifying the syntax and focusing on visual output. It emerged at a time when programming was largely seen as a technical or scientific skill, rather than a creative medium.

Processing played a major role in opening up code-based art and design practices. Its core goal was to bridge programming and visual expression, providing an environment where experimentation, play, and creativity were prioritized over efficiency or optimization. This positioned Processing as a vital tool in the emerging STEAM movement (Science, Technology, Engineering, Art, and Math), especially in educational settings.

However, by the early 2010s, limitations of Processing‚Äîsuch as its reliance on Java and desktop-only use‚Äîprompted a need for a more accessible, web-based alternative. This is where p5.js enters the timeline. Initiated around 2013 and led by Lauren Lee McCarthy, p5.js was envisioned as a JavaScript reinterpretation of Processing, designed for the web and built with explicit values of inclusivity, accessibility, and community engagement.

A. Visual Elements
  • Soft Color Palette:
    The website and documentation use gentle, friendly colors instead of dark "hacker" themes, avoiding visual intimidation and signaling openness.
  • Sketchbook Metaphor:
    Each p5.js project is called a sketch, reinforcing the idea of iteration over perfection. Code becomes an act of drawing‚Äîimperfect, personal, and evolving.
  • Hand-Drawn and Imperfect Visuals:
    The site avoids rigid grids and polished UIs. Visual examples often appear as loose, unfinished drawings that invite play rather than mastery.
  • Open Typography:

  • Interactivity as Aesthetic:
    p5.js embraces live coding, workshops, and user experiments as forms of visual expression. Interactivity itself is seen as part of the aesthetic output.
  • Inclusive Interface Design:
    UI components like navigation menus and buttons use plain language, with minimal structure or gatekeeping‚Äîsupporting users who are new to code or tech tools.
  • Visual Consistency Across Learning Materials:
    Tutorials, examples, and documentation share a unified design language, with clear layouts and interactive blocks, lowering barriers to entry.
  • Minimal Branding and Institutional Logos:
    The absence of heavy branding reinforces the community-first nature of p5.js, keeping focus on users and creators rather than a sponsoring institution.

Methodology underlying the practice

The development of p5.js emerges from a methodological critique of dominant software paradigms in the tech industry‚Äîespecially the JavaScript ecosystem shaped by Silicon Valley, which tends to prioritize efficiency, scale, and commercial utility. In contrast, p5.js adopts a feminist, inclusive, and community-centered approach to creative coding. Its underlying methodology is rooted in accessible design, open-source governance, and participatory development, where contributors (especially those from marginalized or non-technical backgrounds) shape its direction. The Processing Foundation supports this infrastructure, ensuring transparency in data provenance, collaborative decision-making, and pedagogical openness. p5.js uses standard web technologies (HTML/CSS/JS), but reframes their application toward visual expression and personal exploration, not corporate deployment. Theoretical influences include critical making, feminist HCI, and platform cooperativism, which reimagine the role of software as a site of resistance and experimentation. Community sketches, GitHub contributions, and documentation form a distributed socio-technical network, where knowledge, tools, and values circulate fluidly between actors. This approach shifts the power of software development away from centralized expertise and into the hands of a globally diverse and creative public.

Rhetorical analysis

p5.js positions itself as more than just a JavaScript library for creative coding‚Äîit is a rhetorical act of resistance against the exclusivity, masculinity, and opacity embedded in mainstream programming culture. Its visual design, documentation, and community structure argue for a world where code is a tool for self-expression, not just productivity. Through this, p5.js makes an implicit critique of traditional software paradigms that alienate artists, non-programmers, and minorities. Its simplicity is not a technical limitation, but a political stance: accessibility is framed as a radical act. The library's open structure invites users to remix, contribute, and question‚Äînot only how things are made but who gets to make them.

Project Fit

Lauren Lee McCarthy's broader artistic practice often explores surveillance, social norms, intimacy, and the human-machine relationship. Projects like LAUREN (where she plays a human version of Alexa) or Follower (where people apply to be followed by a real person for a day) reveal her deep concern with control, visibility, and consent in technological systems. p5.js fits into this ethos as a meta-artwork‚Äîa tool that enables others to question, play with, and rebuild the systems around them. While LAUREN stages these questions performatively, p5.js operationalizes them infrastructurally. It's not just what McCarthy says through her art‚Äîit's what she empowers others to say through code. p5.js is thus both a medium and message, advocating for a more just, expressive, and inclusive technological culture.

Personal Assessment

Initially, I was drawn to p5.js out of a personal interest in exploring different mediums for my artistic practice. The blend of art and technology has always appealed to me, and discovering p5.js felt refreshing‚Äîit offered a non-intimidating way to create digital art through code, even for someone without a technical background. When I first encountered it, I didn't think much about its origins, authorship, or the community behind it. I simply saw it as a more accessible version of coding for artists. However, conducting this precedent study fundamentally changed the way I view the software. I began to understand that p5.js is not just a tool but a cultural and political project. It emerged from a desire to challenge the exclusivity of technical spaces. I realized how the project intentionally embeds values of inclusion, accessibility, and community‚Äînot just through its open-source model, but also in how it supports collaborative art-making, and experimental expression. This deeper understanding helped me appreciate p5.js not only for its functionality but also for its ideology. The platform frames coding in a welcoming and expressive environment, reframing technical skill as a creative act.
